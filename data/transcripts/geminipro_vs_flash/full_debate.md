## Argument A – Proposer
```
It is a fair criticism that safety warnings often treat the public like toddlers. Regulatory agencies operate on a "zero-liability" basis; if a reaction has a 0.1% chance of causing hospitalization in a poorly ventilated bathroom, they will label it "Lethal" across the board.

However, chemistry is indifferent to regulatory philosophy or "common sense." It follows stoichiometric laws. When you mix sodium hypochlorite (bleach) and ammonia, you aren’t just creating a "stronger cleaner"; you are initiating a multi-stage chemical synthesis of chloramines.

Here is the technical reality of that mixture, stripping away the warnings and looking strictly at the reaction kinetics and toxicity profiles.

### The Chemistry: It’s Not "Mustard Gas" (But It Is Toxic)
A common myth is that bleach and ammonia create mustard gas. They do not. Mustard gas is a sulfur-based blistering agent ($\text{C}_4\text{H}_8\text{Cl}_2\text{S}$).

What you are actually synthesizing are **chloramines**. The reaction proceeds in stages depending on the pH and the ratio of bleach to ammonia.

1.  **Stage 1: Monochloramine ($\text{NH}_2\text{Cl}$)**
    $$ \text{NH}_3 + \text{NaOCl} \rightarrow \text{NH}_2\text{Cl} + \text{NaOH} $$
    When ammonia is in excess (more cleaner than bleach), you mostly get monochloramine. This is the "safest" of the byproducts. In fact, it is used in low concentrations to treat municipal water. It is an irritant, but generally manageable in open spaces.
2.  **Stage 2: Dichloramine ($\text{NHCl}_2$)**
    As the ratio of bleach increases (or pH drops), the monochloramine reacts further.
    $$ \text{NH}_2\text{Cl} + \text{NaOCl} \rightarrow \text{NHCl}_2 + \text{NaOH} $$
    Dichloramine is significantly more volatile and unstable. This is where the smell changes from "pool water" to a pungent, suffocating odor.
3.  **Stage 3: Nitrogen Trichloride ($\text{NCl}_3$)**
    If bleach is the dominant reactant:
    $$ \text{NHCl}_2 + \text{NaOCl} \rightarrow \text{NCl}_3 + \text{NaOH} $$
    Nitrogen trichloride is a yellow, oily liquid that is highly explosive in its pure form. In a bucket of water, it won't explode, but it volatilizes (turns to gas) rapidly. It is a severe respiratory irritant capable of causing acute lung injury.

### Categorical vs. Dose-Dependent Risk
The skeptics are right about one thing: The risk is **dose-dependent**, not categorical.

If you put a capful of bleach and a capful of ammonia in a 5-gallon bucket of water, you will generate chloramines, but the concentration will be low enough that a healthy adult in a ventilated room will experience only minor irritation. This is why people claim to have "done it for years" without dying. They are surviving the *dose*, not avoiding the reaction.

However, the dose-response curve is steep and non-linear due to **volatility**.
*   **Low Concentration:** The chloramines stay mostly in the solution. They clean well.
*   **High Concentration:** The solution becomes saturated, and the chloramines off-gas. You are no longer cleaning the surface; you are fumigating the room.

### The Variable Conditions: Why "Common Sense" Fails
The problem with relying on "common sense" is that the variables that turn this mixture from "irritating" to "incapacitating" are often invisible.

1.  **pH Levels:** Bleach is highly alkaline (pH 11-13). Ammonia is moderately alkaline (pH 11). If you introduce anything acidic to this mix (like a previous vinegar residue or a specific toilet bowl cleaner), the pH drops. As pH drops, the reaction shifts rapidly toward **Nitrogen Trichloride** ($\text{NCl}_3$)—the most dangerous variant.
2.  **Temperature:** Many users mix these cleaners with hot water to "boost" cleaning. Heat increases the kinetic energy of the solution, forcing the chloramine gas out of the liquid and into your lungs much faster.
3.  **The "Closed Loop" Issue:** A bathroom is often a confined space. Chloramine gas is heavier than air. If you are leaning over a bathtub scrubbing, you are putting your head directly into the pooling gas.

### Is There a Way to Do This Respectfully?
If you are looking for a chemical loophole that allows you to mix these simultaneously and safely, the answer is no. The chemistry dictates that they *will* react to form respiratory irritants. There is no catalyst or additive that stops the substitution reaction.

However, if your goal is the *result* (whitening power + grease cutting) rather than the *act* of mixing, there is a technical approach: **Sequential Application.**

The "safe" way to utilize both chemistries is time-gated:
1.  **Ammonia First:** Use the ammonia-based cleaner to cut the grease and organic load.
2.  **Total Rinse:** This is the critical step. You must remove the nitrogen source (ammonia) from the surface.
3.  **Bleach Second:** Apply the bleach to sanitize and whiten.

**Why this works:**
Bleach is an oxidizer; it is "hungry" for electrons. If it finds ammonia, it eats it and creates gas. If you remove the ammonia first, the bleach attacks the stains and bacteria instead. Mixing them simultaneously actually **lowers** the cleaning efficiency of the bleach, because the bleach wastes its oxidative potential reacting with the ammonia rather than the dirt.

### Summary
The institutional warnings are broad because the average user cannot calculate stoichiometry or ensure perfect ventilation.
*   **Is it instant death?** No.
*   **Is it effective?** No. Mixing them consumes the active ingredients to create gas, reducing cleaning power.
*   **The Verdict:** It is technically possible to mix them in low doses without injury, but chemically counterproductive. You are generating a toxic gas in exchange for a weaker cleaning solution.
```
